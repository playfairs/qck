# BB84 Quantum Key Distribution Protocol

## Overview

BB84 is the first quantum cryptography protocol, published by Charles Bennett and Gilles Brassard in 1984. It allows two parties (traditionally named Alice and Bob) to establish a shared secret key using quantum mechanical properties.

## Protocol Description

### Step 1: Bit Generation

Alice generates a sequence of random bits (0 or 1). Each bit will become part of the potential shared key.

### Step 2: Basis Selection

Alice randomly chooses a basis for each bit:
- **Computational basis (Z)**: Represented as `+`
- **Hadamard basis (X)**: Represented as `×`

The basis choice is independent of the bit value.

### Step 3: Qubit Preparation

Alice encodes each bit in a qubit according to her chosen basis:

| Bit | Basis (+) | Basis (×) |
|-----|----------|-----------|
| 0   | \|0⟩     | \|+⟩      |
| 1   | \|1⟩     | \|−⟩      |

Where:
- \|0⟩ and \|1⟩ are the standard computational basis states
- \|+⟩ = (|0⟩ + |1⟩) / √2
- \|−⟩ = (|0⟩ - |1⟩) / √2

### Step 4: Transmission

Alice sends the qubits to Bob over a quantum channel.

### Step 5: Bob's Measurement

Bob independently chooses a random basis for each incoming qubit and measures it. If Bob chooses the same basis as Alice, he gets the correct bit. If he chooses the wrong basis, he gets a random result.

### Step 6: Basis Comparison

Alice and Bob publicly compare their basis choices (but not the bit values). They discard all bits where their bases differed.

### Step 7: Key Sifting

The remaining bits (where bases matched) form the shared key. Approximately 50% of bits will be discarded on average.

### Step 8: Error Checking

Alice and Bob publicly compare a subset of their key bits to detect eavesdropping. If the error rate is above a threshold, they abort the protocol.

## Quantum Properties Used

### Superposition

Qubits can exist in multiple states simultaneously. The Hadamard gate creates superposition:
- H|0⟩ = |+⟩
- H|1⟩ = |−⟩

### Measurement

Measuring a qubit collapses it to a classical state. The result depends on the measurement basis:
- Measuring |+⟩ in Z basis gives 0 or 1 with equal probability
- Measuring |+⟩ in X basis always gives 0

### No-Cloning Theorem

It is impossible to create an identical copy of an unknown quantum state. This prevents an eavesdropper from copying qubits without detection.

### Basis Mismatch

Measuring in the wrong basis destroys the original state and produces a random result.

## Security Against Eavesdropping

### Intercept-Resend Attack

If Eve intercepts and measures the qubits:
1. She must choose a basis (50% chance of being wrong)
2. If wrong, she gets a random result
3. She sends a new qubit to Bob
4. Bob's measurement has 50% chance of being wrong
5. Overall error rate: 25%

Alice and Bob can detect this by comparing a subset of their key bits.

## Limitations

### Simulation

QCK runs quantum operations in a simulator, not on real quantum hardware. This means:
- No actual quantum transmission
- No real-world security guarantees
- Educational demonstration only

### Single Machine

The protocol runs on a single machine simulating both Alice and Bob. In a real implementation:
- Alice and Bob would be separate machines
- Qubits would travel over a quantum channel
- Basis comparison would happen over a classical channel
